Small Commercial Building Energy Analysis

30-day load profile analysis with a simulated 30 kW rooftop solar PV scenario

Prepared by Rohan Ramjutton

Project Overview

This project analyses the electricity usage of a simulated small commercial building over a 30-day period. An Excel-based dashboard was developed to calculate total energy use, peak demand, estimated electricity cost, emissions, overnight base load, solar self-consumption, exported solar energy, grid import after solar and simple payback.

The aim was to understand how building load data can be used to assess energy behaviour and support early-stage rooftop solar PV feasibility decisions.

Methodology

The analysis was completed using a 30-day half-hourly electricity demand dataset for a simulated small commercial building. The dataset was processed in Excel to convert demand readings into interval energy use, identify daily load behaviour, estimate grid electricity costs and compare the original demand profile with a simplified rooftop solar PV scenario.

1. Load Data Processing

Each electricity demand reading was provided in kilowatts (kW) at 30-minute intervals. To calculate energy use for each interval, the demand value was multiplied by the interval duration of 0.5 hours:

Energy (kWh) = Demand (kW) × 0.5 h

The half-hourly energy values were then summed to calculate the total 30-day energy consumption and daily energy usage. Peak demand was identified by finding the maximum demand value recorded during the analysis period.

2. Base Load and Daily Profile Analysis

The building load profile was reviewed to identify how demand changed throughout the day. Overnight demand was used to estimate the building's base load, representing electricity consumption that remains present outside normal operating hours. This helped separate constant background demand from daytime operational demand.

3. Solar PV Scenario Modelling

A simplified 30 kW rooftop solar PV generation profile was modelled to represent typical daytime solar production. The solar generation profile was compared against the building demand at each half-hourly interval to determine how much solar energy could be used directly by the building.

For each interval, the available solar generation was split into two outcomes:

  • Solar self-consumption: solar energy used directly by the building.
  • Exported solar energy: surplus solar generation exported when solar production exceeded building demand.

4. Grid Import and Export Calculation

Grid import after solar was calculated by subtracting usable solar generation from the building demand at each interval. When building demand was greater than solar generation, the remaining demand was supplied by the grid. When solar generation exceeded demand, the excess was recorded as exported energy.

  • Grid import after solar: building demand not supplied by solar PV.
  • Exported solar: solar generation remaining after building demand was met.
  • Solar self-consumption percentage: solar used on-site divided by total solar generation.

5. Cost, Emissions and Payback Estimation

Electricity cost was estimated by applying an assumed grid electricity tariff to imported energy. Solar savings were estimated based on the reduction in grid-imported electricity after the 30 kW solar PV system was applied. An emissions factor was also used to estimate the reduction in grid-related carbon emissions.

A simple payback period was calculated by comparing the assumed installed cost of the solar PV system against the estimated financial benefit from reduced grid electricity consumption.

Simple Payback = Estimated Solar System Cost ÷ Estimated Annual Solar Benefit

6. Dashboard Development

The final Excel dashboard was built to summarise the key technical and financial outputs of the analysis. It includes total energy consumption, peak demand, grid import after solar, exported energy, solar self-consumption, estimated cost reduction, emissions reduction and simple payback. The dashboard was designed to present the results in a clear format suitable for early-stage energy feasibility assessment.

Assumptions and Limitations

  • The load data is simulated for a small commercial building.
  • Electricity demand is recorded in 30-minute intervals.
  • The 30 kW rooftop solar PV system is modelled using a simplified daytime generation curve.
  • Tariffs, demand charges, emissions factor and solar system cost are assumed for concept-level analysis.
  • The analysis does not include detailed electrical design, inverter losses, shading, weather variation, protection design, grid connection approval or roof structural assessment.
